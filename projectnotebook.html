WHOOP: A Journey Into Unocking Human Performance¶

Research Question:¶

Does Whoop allow the user to "unlock their human performance" as specified in their mission statement?¶

Introduction¶

This early phases of this project began months ago when Ben decided to purchase a whoop. A whoop is a fitness wearable that claims to improve the health and performance of it's wearers by utilizing an array of biomarkers regarding sleep and fitness. On the company's site, whoop.com, Whoop claims to reduce the wearers heart rate, increase their heart rate variability, and improve their sleep in a significant manner after four months. Our goal is to see if we have been able to reap these benefits, after using the product.

If, after four months, Ben's heart rate decreases 4.4 beats per minute, heart rate variability increases by 8 milliseconds, and sleep improves by 41 minutes, then we can assume that by Whoop's definition, Ben has unlocked his human performance. The question that remains will be if Ben feels that way as well.

After collecting, analyzing and calculating significance with our data set, it was determined that Ben's biomarkers did not improve in the way that Whoop articulated they would. Because of this, we fail to prove that Ben was able to "unlock his human performance" through Whoop's definition. However, through this experience Ben has developed a better understanding of his biomarkers and how they are affected by his lifestyle choices. Although his human potential wasn't necessarily unlocked, through this experience, Ben feels that he at least knows where he could find the key.

Preregistered Analyses¶

  • Strain and Calories: We predict strain and calories will be positively correlated.

  • Resting Heart Rate and Recovery: We predict heart rate increasing will negatively correlate with recovery score.

The app allows us to import the Whoop data into a CSV file by passing our login information through the API. This is named rawwhoop.csv, and is saved to our github.

Data Description¶

What are the observations (rows) and the attributes (columns)?¶

The observations are days in chronological order, and the attributes are a variety of measured and calculated recordings taken from Whoop's API like sleep duration, average heart rate, max heart rate, and respiratory rate.

Why was this dataset created?¶

This dataset was created due to Ben's curiosity into his body's functions throughout each day. While the Whoop provides an ergonomic GUI for analyzing the data, the question remained if there was any analysis being missed by the app. By analyzing the raw csv, we can see for ourselves if there are any relationships not displayed by the GUI.

Who funded the creation of the dataset?¶

The creation of this dataset was funded by Ben. He pays $30 per month for the Whoop membership, which comes with the free wearable that tracks strain, recovery, and sleep by using an extremely accurate heartrate and movement sensor, using LEDs and a 3 axis accelerometer.

What processes might have influenced what data was observed and recorded and what was not?¶

The Whoop strap 3.0 has a 5 day battery, is waterproof, and has no interface. It is designed to never be taken off. Because of the nature of this 24/7 data collection, there are no gaps in the observed data, and therefore no disgression on our behalf when choosing what data to include in our set. That being said, there is the inherent issue that the designers of Whoop's software have some say in what gets factored into calculating the various markers, recovery, strain, sleep score etc. Their process of data gathering and cleaning would be the single largest variable in determining what was recorded.

What preprocessing was done, and how did the data come to be in the form that you are using?¶

We located a third party app online that scraped the Whoop HTML app, and converted the data into an excel file, which we were then able to save as a CSV. There was no preprocessing done by the software, however the preprocessing done by Whoop themselves was significant. For example, in order to calculate strain, the app uses a number of datapoints such as heart rate, and heart rate variability, both recorded multiple times a second by the device. The raw datapoints are available to the user to some degree, but there is a significant amount of calculations done on the back-end to reach strain, sleep, and recovery scores.

If people are involved, were they aware of the data collection and if so, what purpose did they expect the data to be used for?¶

Ben was aware of the data being collected and even funding it. In fact, one of the variables measured is recovery score. This is measured through a function that computes resting heart rate, sleep in hours, and a host of other variables that would give a scaled number from 1 to 100 that represents the recovery from the previous day's physical strain. He used Whoop to optimize physical recovery and growth, thus as he viewed his data being collected he altered his daily choices regarding physical activity. For example, on a day where Ben woke up with a 90 percent recovery score, he would be more inclined to go for a challenging run, or perhaps increase the amount of weight he trained with. Vice Versa, on a 15 percent recovery day, Ben would be encouraged to take an ice bath or nap. This is an essential part of "Unlocking Human Performance" as advertised by Whoop. Their hypothesis is that by understanding your body's physiological markers, you can better tailor your activities to achieve peak performance. One of the biggest questions we aim to answer is whether or not this is actually the case.

Was any preprocessing/cleaning/labeling of the data done?¶

Yes there was preprocessing done to the data. We created a cleaning function as opposed to cleaning the set by hand which allows us to update our project with more data collected by whoop until the day it is due. The cleaning function formats the titles to lowercase, and strips outer white space. Then, it discards the workouts, comments, hrv and sleep duration columns. The reason for discarding the workouts and comments columns are due to a quirk in the API used to collect data, and these columns were completely empty or contained 0 for every entry. The hrv and sleep duration columns are dropped because this data is already stored in other columns in a more readable manner, hrv (in milliseconds) and sleep (hours) respectively.

Data Limitations¶

Identify any potential problems with your dataset.

Problems with the data set include the accuracy of the data as measured by the Whoop Strap 3.0. The device potentially mismeasured the data that we collected. However, we expect this data to be accurate and there is no reason to suspect that there are inaccuracies in the dataset. Additionally, It is difficult, however possible, to prove or disprove our research question due to the limitations of what the Whoop Strap 3.0 can measure. Intially, this was considered a data limitation; however, Whoop quanitfies 'unlocking your human performance'. According to Whoop.com, unlocking your human performance consists of reducing resting heart rate (RHR) by 4.4 BPM, heart rate variablilty (HRV) by 8 milliseconds, and increasing sleep by 41 minutes per night. Thus, we can quantify 'unlocking your human perfomance'.

Another important aspect to look at is the fact that Ben looked at his data everyday. This influenced his daily decisions regarding physical activity. These daily decisions that Ben made influenced the future collection of data. For example, after a day of hiking Ben's strain score was high the next morning. Therefore, he decided to take the day off which influenced data collection.

Ben was infleunced by his ability to look at his data everyday, but this doesn't thwart the data's validity. The Whoop Strap 3.0 is made for the purpose of training and recovering effectively, which is defined by your strain and recovery score, respectively. When Ben looks at his daily recovery score and makes desicions regarding his exersize for the day, Ben is optimizing his training. Ben is acting in his own self interest as any reasonable economic player would. If done properly, Whoop claims on their website that after four months users will experience a litany of positive health benefits like decresed resting heart rate, better sleep, and fewer injuries.

There are few, if any, data limitations for our data set given the accuracy and purpose of the Whoop Strap 3.0.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
def plotandcorrelate(x,y,title):
    xlabel = str(x.name)
    ylabel = str(y.name)
    plt.xlabel(xlabel)
    plt.ylabel(ylabel)
    plt.title(title)
    plt.scatter(x,y)
    plt.show()
    print("The correlation between {} and {} is: ".format(xlabel,ylabel) + str(x.corr(y)))
    print("The covariance between {} and {} is: ".format(xlabel,ylabel) + str(x.cov(y)))

Despite having a low correlation, a linear regression was created to attempt to predict strain scores based on the previous night's sleep score. While the regression line has a slightly positive slope, meaning that a higher sleep score will predict a slightly higher strain, ultimately the coefficient of determination for the regression was very low. To test the predicting ability of the regression, the data for strain and sleep score was split into two parts, a test and training set. The model was trained using the training set, and then attempted to predict the Y values in the test set based on the X values in the test set, which ultimately scored a 0.08 coefficient of determination. This implies that although higher strains and higher sleep scores may correlate together, predicting human activity in as high of a fidelity as we are is a task that most likely requires more sophisticated analyses than a linear regression. Evaluation of Significance¶

To evaluate the significance of this data, we will be comparing the first 30 days of Ben's whoop data to his last 30 days. We will test for signficance with heart rate, heart rate variability, and sleep. We chose these markers because Whoop identifies these as indicators of increases in general health, and claims to improve these metrics as a result of purchasing the device. If we prove that Ben has improved in these markers to a significant degree, I.E. more than what could be accredited to random variation, then we can say with confidence that Ben has "unlocked his human performance" by Whoop's definition.

Interpretation and Conclusions¶

Throughout our pre registered analysis question and data analysis, we were not surprised to find a strong correlation between some variables like calories and strain score. Additionally, we more or less correctly predicted which variables will have strong correlations and which will have weaker correlations. This analysis is available above. We are very confident in these conclusions given the accuracy of the Whoop strap, and the size of the samples that we were able to collect. These results affirm our suspicions that a variety of variables are used in the computation of an overall strain score, recovery score, and sleep performance. Of all of the relationships that we examined, the most suprising was the correlation between strain, calories, and max heart rate, or average heart rate. The correlations we discovered led us to believe that for Ben's physiology, high intensity exercise was more effective at increasing the amount of strain and calories he burned in a given day. Another part of the data analysis that we found elucidating was the graphical representation of Ben's sleep for each day of the week with box plots. The representation of how Ben's sleep changed based on his habits on the weekdays or weekends gave us pause, and will most likely lead us to reevaluate our lifestyle choices and put more focus into how we sleep throughout the week.

The three metrics to determine whether Ben has 'achieved his human performance' are resting heart rate, heart rate variability, and sleep (hr). Contrary to what we thought would occur: resting heart rate had no significant change, heart rate variability decreased, and sleep also had no significant change. Therefore, Ben did not unlock his human performance based on the markers set out by Whoop. This is a disheartening conclusion because Ben did commit time and energy into physical activity that would improve physical function as measured by the whoop strap. One important thing to note is that there may be many underlying factors that affected these changes. For example, HRV and recovery score correlate strongly together. It is possible that Ben was overall less recovered in his last month due to putting more emphasis on his training, rather than on his recovery. It is also possible, although improbable, that Ben simply got less healthy over this time period.

Whoop can only do so much as it pertains to measuring data of physical ability, which is inevitably what our conclusion must be. Technology like Whoop, that allows us to get a vivid image of our biology and physiology, is very new, and most certainly an imperfect science. Although based on Whoop's metrics, Ben may have gotten unhealthier, we have reason to believe that the opposite is true. For example, Ben increased his squat from 275 pounds for three reps to 330 for three reps over the duration of this data collection. In the bench press, Ben started at 255 pounds for one rep to 265 pounds for three reps. While Ben's biomarkers may not have improved, what did improve was his relationship with exercise, functional fitness, and living a happier and healthier life. Whoop led to a change in mindset: it was not necessarily important how you increased your strain for a given day. It could be dancing, running, lifting weights or riding a bike, and it was all the same to the algorithm. What was important, no matter what the activity was, was elevating the heart rate, sleeping more, drinking less, and choosing healthier habits overall. After experiencing Whoop, this was Ben's final takeaway. While optimizing human performance sounds complex, and daunting, all it really means is to choose healthier lifestyle options, keep your heart rate elevated for a significant period of time every day, and understand your body's physiology in a more nuanced way.

Although Ben failed to unlock his human potential through Whoop's definition, in his own definition, he feels that he has.
